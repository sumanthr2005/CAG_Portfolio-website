Activity 3: OTEC Power System

Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion (OTEC) utilizes the temperature difference between warm surface seawater and cold deep seawater to generate electricity. The provided diagram represents a closed-cycle OTEC system, where a working fluid such as ammonia circulates within a sealed system. Warm seawater evaporates the fluid in an evaporator, the vapor drives a turbine-generator to produce electricity, and then cold deep seawater condenses the vapor back into liquid form, completing the cycle.

Working Principle: The continuous cycle of evaporation and condensation based on the ocean‚Äôs thermal gradient ensures steady power generation. The minimum temperature difference required is about 20¬∞C between the surface and deep seawater layers.

Advantages: Continuous 24/7 operation, renewable and clean energy source, and potential for combined freshwater and hydrogen production.
Disadvantages: High capital cost, complex underwater infrastructure, and location constraints (requires tropical regions with stable temperature gradients).

Site Selection Criteria: Best suited for tropical coastal regions where surface water temperatures are around 25‚Äì30¬∞C and deep-water temperatures are about 5¬∞C. Examples include areas near Hawaii, India, and the Caribbean.

Applications: Apart from electricity generation, OTEC systems can provide desalinated freshwater, cold seawater air-conditioning (SWAC), and support aquaculture. It holds promise for sustainable island and coastal community development.

Challenging Questions & Answers

  • Q1: What are the main thermodynamic limitations in achieving high efficiency in OTEC systems?
    A1: Since the temperature difference is small (typically 20¬∞C), the Carnot efficiency is limited to about 6‚Äì7%. This requires large working fluid volumes and heat exchangers to maintain viable output.
  • Q2: How can deep seawater pipe designs be optimized?
    A2: Lightweight composite pipes with flexible joints and optimized hydrodynamic shape reduce frictional losses and structural stress, improving cold-water delivery efficiency.
  • Q3: What factors influence the selection of working fluids in closed-cycle OTEC systems?
    A3: Fluids are chosen based on boiling point, thermal stability, environmental impact, and availability. Ammonia and R-134a are common choices due to low boiling temperatures and efficiency.
  • Q4: How can OTEC systems be hybridized for higher viability?
    A4: By coupling with desalination plants, aquaculture cooling systems, or hydrogen production units, OTEC can provide multiple outputs‚Äîelectricity, freshwater, and cooling‚Äîenhancing overall efficiency.
  • Q5: What are the long-term environmental impacts of large-scale OTEC deployment?
    A5: While generally low, possible impacts include changes in local marine temperature and nutrient mixing. Careful outflow management and environmental monitoring minimize these risks.
