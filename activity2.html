Activity 2: Biomass-Based Biogas Generation System

Biogas production is a sustainable method of converting organic waste into renewable energy through anaerobic digestion. The diagram shows the working of a biogas plant designed for a dairy farm handling cattle waste. Organic waste, such as dung and slurry, is fed into a mixing pit and then sent into digester tanks where bacteria decompose the biomass in the absence of oxygen, producing a mixture of methane (CH‚ÇÑ) and carbon dioxide (CO‚ÇÇ).

Working Process: The produced biogas is collected at the top of the digester and can be used for electricity generation, heating, or cooking. The remaining slurry, called fermentation residue, serves as a nutrient-rich fertilizer. Heat exchangers maintain optimal temperatures inside the digester to ensure efficient microbial activity.

Advantages: Reduces dependence on fossil fuels, converts waste to energy, provides organic fertilizer, and reduces greenhouse gas emissions.
Disadvantages: Requires constant feedstock supply, moderate setup costs, and proper temperature control for efficiency.

Site Selection Criteria: Availability of biomass (animal dung, agricultural residues, food waste), water, and suitable land. Proximity to power distribution or consumption points enhances viability.

Applications: Biogas plants are widely used in rural areas for household energy, electricity generation for farms, and powering small industries. Large-scale systems contribute to municipal waste management and decentralized power generation.

  • Q2: How does the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio affect efficiency?
    A2: An imbalanced C/N ratio leads to process inhibition‚Äîtoo much carbon slows digestion, while excess nitrogen causes ammonia toxicity. Proper feedstock mixing maintains equilibrium for optimal microbial activity.
  • Q3: What are the challenges in scaling biogas systems to community levels?
    A3: Issues include high setup cost, irregular feedstock supply, and maintenance challenges. Community-level management, feedstock collection logistics, and government support can overcome these.
  • Q4: How can slurry byproduct improve agriculture?
    A4: The digested slurry is rich in nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, enhancing soil fertility. It also reduces dependence on chemical fertilizers and improves crop yield sustainably.
  • Q5: What innovations can improve gas purification and compression?
    A5: Advanced scrubbing with zeolites or membranes, pressure swing adsorption, and compact gas storage systems can make purified biogas (bio-CNG) more efficient and economical.
